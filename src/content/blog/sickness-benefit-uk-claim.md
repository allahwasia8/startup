## Key Takeaways

<ul><li><p>Sickness benefit in the UK usually starts with statutory sick pay from your employer, then may move to employment and support allowance or universal credit with a health element if you remain unable to work.</p></li><li><p>If you have a health condition or disability that gives you limited capability for work, you may get extra money through Universal Credit (LCW / LCWRA) or new style employment and support allowance.</p></li><li><p>You can self-certify sickness for the first 7 days and then need a fit note from your GP or other authorised medical professional from day 8.</p></li><li><p>People on a low income or with no income can claim universal credit, and fit notes, Work Capability Assessments and the claimant commitment are central parts of the process.</p></li><li><p>Special rules apply if a medical professional expects you to have 12 months or less to live, giving faster decisions and higher rates of financial support.</p></li></ul>

## Introduction: What Are Sickness Benefits in the UK?

<p>UK sickness benefits provide financial support when a health condition or disability stops or limits your ability to work for more than a few days. Whether you have a short-term illness or a lifelong health condition, these benefits exist to make sure you are not left without income while you recover or adjust.</p><p>This article covers the current rules as of June 2026 in England, Scotland and Wales. Some details differ in Northern Ireland, so check locally if that applies to you.</p><p>Sickness benefits are for temporary inability to work due to illness, and claiming sickness benefits in the UK typically involves two stages: claiming SSP and applying for ESA or Universal Credit. The main schemes you need to know about are:</p><ul><li><p><strong>Statutory Sick Pay (SSP)</strong> - paid by your employer when you first fall ill.</p></li><li><p><strong>New style Employment and Support Allowance (ESA)</strong> - a contributory benefit for those with enough national insurance contributions.</p></li><li><p><strong>Universal Credit</strong> - means-tested support that includes extra elements for health conditions limiting work.</p></li><li><p><strong>Disability benefits</strong> such as personal independence payment (PIP) and attendance allowance, which help with the extra costs of living with a disability or health condition.</p></li></ul><p>Later sections cover eligibility, how to get a fit note, how the work capability assessment works, the claimant commitment, and what to do if you are nearing the end of life.</p>

## Quick Check: Which Sickness Benefit Should You Look At First?

<p>This section gives you a fast way to see what to claim based on your circumstances. Eligibility for sickness benefits depends on employment status, so start here:</p><ul><li><p><strong>Employed and recently off sick:</strong> Check entitlement to statutory sick pay through your employer. The claim for SSP must be made to the employer directly.</p></li><li><p><strong>SSP ending after 28 weeks or you do not qualify:</strong> Look at new style employment and support allowance. If SSP runs out, individuals can apply for new style ESA.</p></li><li><p><strong>Low income or no income (including self-employed or unemployed):</strong> Check Universal Credit with a health condition declared. Universal Credit is a means-tested benefit available for those with low income or high housing costs.</p></li><li><p><strong>Long-term health condition or disability affecting day-to-day living:</strong> Check personal independence payment pip or attendance allowance alongside other benefits.</p></li></ul><p>You may sometimes claim more than one type at the same time. For example, you can receive new style ESA plus Universal Credit together, but payments are adjusted so you are not paid twice for the same need. ESA is deducted pound-for-pound from your universal credit payment.</p><blockquote><p>Use an up-to-date benefits calculator and, where possible, independent advice from citizens advice or a welfare rights adviser to confirm your exact entitlement.</p></blockquote>

## Overview of Sickness Benefits and How They Fit Together

<p>The UK system combines contributory benefits (based on National Insurance) and means-tested benefits to support people with health conditions. Here is how the key schemes fit together:</p><ul><li><p><strong>Statutory sick pay (SSP)</strong> is the first step for most employees who become ill. It is paid by your employer for up to 28 weeks.</p></li><li><p><strong>New style employment and support allowance (ESA)</strong> is a contributory benefit for those with sufficient national insurance contributions who are unable to work due to a health condition or disability.</p></li><li><p><strong>Universal Credit</strong> is means-tested financial support, including extra elements for limited capability for work or work-related activity.</p></li><li><p><strong>Disability benefits</strong> such as PIP and attendance allowance help with extra living costs, not income replacement.</p></li></ul><p>Sickness benefits include statutory sick pay and employment support allowance. Disability benefits include personal independence payment and attendance allowance. You can claim both sickness and disability benefits simultaneously because they serve different purposes - sickness benefits replace lost income, while disability benefits support long-term health conditions or disabilities by covering additional daily living costs.</p><p>The "sickness route" in 2026 mainly means ESA and Universal Credit for people whose health condition limits work. These have replaced older benefits like Incapacity Benefit and income support for most new claimants.</p>

## Statutory Sick Pay (SSP): First-Line Support for Employees

<p>SSP is what most employees receive from their employer when they are too ill to work. From 6 April 2026, the rules were reformed significantly under the Employment Rights Act 2025.</p><p><strong>Eligibility:</strong></p><ul><li><p>You must be classed as an employee. Self-employed people cannot claim SSP.</p></li><li><p>From 6 April 2026, SSP is payable from day one of sickness - the old 3-day waiting period and the Lower Earnings Limit have been removed for most new sickness absences. However, employees must have average weekly earnings of at least £129 to qualify for SSP under transitional rules that still apply in certain cases. Previously, Statutory Sick Pay (SSP) was £95.85 per week, but this was the older rate.</p></li><li><p>Some workers on agency or zero-hours contracts can qualify, provided they are classed as employed for tax purposes.</p></li></ul><p><strong>Payment basics:</strong></p><ul><li><p>SSP provides £123.25 per week or 80% of average earnings for up to 28 weeks. If 80% of your average weekly earnings is less than £123.25, you receive the lower of the two.</p></li><li><p>It is a flat-rate benefit and not earnings-related beyond the 80% cap.</p></li><li><p>Your employer may offer contractual sick pay that is higher than SSP, which can sit on top of or instead of the statutory amount.</p></li><li><p>Linked periods of sickness (returning to work then falling ill again within 8 weeks) count together toward the 28-week maximum.</p></li></ul><p><strong>Evidence:</strong></p><ul><li><p>Self-certification is required for the first 7 days of illness. You can self-certify for the first seven days of illness without needing a doctor's note.</p></li><li><p>A fit note is needed after 7 days of being unfit for work. This sick note must come from a GP or other approved medical professional.</p></li><li><p>You must provide medical evidence after seven days of illness to continue receiving SSP.</p></li></ul><p><strong>If your employer refuses SSP:</strong></p><ul><li><p>Ask for form SSP1, which confirms that SSP has ended or been refused. You will need this form to pursue new style ESA or Universal Credit instead.</p></li></ul><img src="/images/blog/7eba2256-4fda-4dfe-8b3d-52dab9c807c4.png" alt="A doctor in a clinic setting is handing a document to a patient, who appears to be receiving important medical evidence related to their health condition or disability. This interaction may be linked to the patient's eligibility for various financial support options, such as disability benefits or universal credit.">

## Employment and Support Allowance (ESA): Income When You Cannot Work

<p>New style employment and support allowance is a benefit for people whose health condition or disability limits their capability for work and who have paid enough national insurance contributions. It replaced contribution-based ESA for new claims and sits separately from Universal Credit, though you can claim both.</p><p>Employment and Support Allowance (ESA) has two parts: contributory and income-related. New style ESA is the contributory version, which is not means tested. Income-related ESA has been largely replaced by Universal Credit for new claimants.</p><p><strong>Eligibility:</strong></p><ul><li><p>You must have a health condition or disability that affects your ability to work and provide a fit note from your GP or other healthcare professional.</p></li><li><p>Eligibility for ESA requires a history of national insurance contributions and a health condition affecting work. You must have worked and paid or been credited with sufficient contributions in the relevant tax years.</p></li><li><p>You usually cannot receive SSP and ESA at the same time, but you may claim ESA as SSP ends.</p></li><li><p>Application for ESA requires personal, medical, and financial details, including National Insurance number and bank details.</p></li></ul><p><strong>The ESA assessment process:</strong></p><ul><li><p>During the initial assessment period, you submit fit notes and receive a basic assessment rate.</p></li><li><p>You are then asked to complete a Capability for Work questionnaire (the work questionnaire known as ESA50) and may attend a work capability assessment.</p></li></ul><p><strong>The two ESA groups:</strong></p><ul><li><p><strong>Work related activity group</strong> (linked to limited capability for work - LCW). You may have some work preparation requirements.</p></li><li><p><strong>Support group</strong> (linked to limited capability for work and work-related activity - LCWRA). You have no requirement to prepare for or look for work.</p></li></ul><p><strong>Interaction with Universal Credit:</strong></p><ul><li><p>If you get both new style ESA and Universal Credit, UC will be reduced pound-for-pound by your ESA payment.</p></li><li><p>ESA is not means tested, but UC is, so savings and your partner's income may affect your universal credit claim but not your style ESA.</p></li><li><p>Claiming both can still be worthwhile because ESA continues even if Universal Credit stops due to changes in household income.</p></li></ul>

## Universal Credit When You Have a Health Condition or Disability

<p>Universal Credit can provide additional support for health conditions if you have a low income or no income and your health condition or disability limits your capability for work. It is the main means-tested benefit for working-age people in the UK.</p><p>Universal Credit replaces several legacy benefits including income-related ESA, income support, and Housing Benefit for most new claims. It has specific rules for when you report sickness.</p><p><strong>Main steps when claiming:</strong></p><ul><li><p>When making a new universal credit claim, you must declare if you have a health condition or disability that affects your work or is expected to last at least 4 weeks.</p></li><li><p>If you already receive Universal Credit, report your medical condition as a change of circumstances via your online account or by phone using the universal credit helpline. You must report any health condition changes to Universal Credit immediately.</p></li></ul><p><strong>Fit notes and self-certification:</strong></p><ul><li><p>You can self-certify for the first 7 days of sickness.</p></li><li><p>From day 8, you must provide a fit note if your condition still limits your capability for work.</p></li><li><p>If you are moving from ESA to Universal Credit in some circumstances, you may not need new fit notes initially.</p></li></ul><p><strong>The Work Capability Assessment (WCA) for Universal Credit:</strong></p><ul><li><p>You will usually be sent a Capability for Work questionnaire (WCA50) - this is required for assessments. A Capability for Work questionnaire (WCA50) is required for assessments.</p></li><li><p>You may be invited to a health assessment in person, by video or by phone, where a health professional considers your capability for work and work related activity.</p></li></ul><p><strong>Possible outcomes:</strong></p><ul><li><p><strong>Fit for work:</strong> You must look for work and meet full work search requirements.</p></li><li><p><strong>Limited capability for work (LCW):</strong> Reduced work preparation requirements. Limited Capability for Work (LCW) affects eligibility for Universal Credit, but most new UC claims after April 2017 do not receive extra money just for LCW, with some transitional exceptions.</p></li><li><p><strong>Limited capability for work and work-related activity (LCWRA):</strong> No requirement to look for or prepare for work. You receive an lcwra extra element in your universal credit payment.</p></li></ul><p>From April 2026, there are higher and lower LCWRA amounts depending on when you claimed and whether you meet the new Severe Conditions Criteria (SCC) or were previously in an ESA support group. New claimants who do not meet SCC receive a lower LCWRA rate, while protected claimants, those who are terminally ill, and those meeting SCC receive the higher rate.</p><blockquote><p>When you first access the GOV.UK portal to manage your claim online, you may encounter security checks. The system is designed to block malicious bots, so it runs a security verification step - you may see a page indicating the uk performing security verification on your connection. The security service checks your browser before granting access, and you might notice a respond ray id reference at the bottom of the page. Once verification successful, you can proceed to your journal and account. If you have trouble, try clearing your browser cache or using a different device.</p></blockquote>

## Your Claimant Commitment and Work Requirements When You're Sick

<p>The claimant commitment is the agreement between you and the Department for Work and Pensions (DWP) setting out what you must do to receive Universal Credit. It covers your work search, work preparation and other responsibilities.</p><p><strong>How a health condition or disability affects your claimant commitment:</strong></p><ul><li><p>Your work coach must consider your medical evidence, fit notes and WCA outcome when setting requirements.</p></li><li><p>Requirements should be reasonable and tailored to your capability for work.</p></li></ul><p><strong>Typical expectations by status:</strong></p><ul><li><p><strong>Fit for work:</strong> You will be expected to search for work up to full-time hours, unless agreed otherwise with your work coach.</p></li><li><p><strong>Limited capability for work:</strong> You do not have to look for work, but you may be asked to do work preparation such as training, updating your CV or attending interviews with your work coach. Limited Capability for Work (LCW) means you can earn some money through permitted activities.</p></li><li><p><strong>Limited capability for work and work-related activity:</strong> You usually have no work or work preparation requirements. If assessed with LCWRA, you won't need to prepare for work. You can work voluntarily without losing LCWRA status as long as you report earnings.</p></li></ul><p><strong>Temporary periods of sickness:</strong></p><ul><li><p>You can usually have up to two short periods (up to 14 days each) of being treated as sick in any 12 months without changing your long-term work requirements, provided you submit fit notes from day 8.</p></li></ul><p>You should challenge your claimant commitment if it does not reflect your health limitations. Use medical evidence and support from advisers where possible. You can ask someone to help manage your claim at any time - a friend, family member, or professional adviser can assist you.</p>

## Limited Capability for Work (LCW) and Limited Capability for Work and Work-Related Activity (LCWRA)

<p>LCW and LCWRA are legal terms used after a work capability assessment to describe how your health condition or disability affects your ability to work. They determine both your work requirements and the extra amount you may receive.</p><p><strong>LCW explained:</strong></p><ul><li><p>You are assessed as having limited capability for work, so you are not required to job search actively.</p></li><li><p>You may still have to take part in work preparation activities, such as training or regular meetings with your work coach.</p></li><li><p>Most new Universal Credit claims after April 2017 do not get extra money just for LCW, although some people who moved from ESA or have older claims may still receive an LCW element.</p></li></ul><p><strong>LCWRA explained:</strong></p><ul><li><p>You are assessed as having limited capability for work and work-related activity, meaning you are not required to search for or prepare for work.</p></li><li><p>You may qualify for higher payments if assessed with LCWRA. The LCWRA element provides additional help with your income.</p></li><li><p>From April 2026, two rates exist:</p><ul><li><p><strong>Protected / higher rate:</strong> LCWRA provides an additional payment of £429.80 monthly. This applies to existing claimants, those who are terminally ill, and those meeting the new Severe Conditions Criteria.</p></li><li><p><strong>Lower rate:</strong> The LCWRA amount can be lower at £217.26 monthly for most new claimants after April 2026. This lower rate is frozen until 2029/30.</p></li></ul></li><li><p>Couples usually get only one LCWRA element even if both partners qualify, paid at the higher applicable rate.</p></li></ul><p><strong>How LCW / LCWRA decisions are made:</strong></p><ul><li><p>DWP uses a set of functional descriptors, such as difficulty walking a certain distance, using your hands, staying conscious or managing treatment. The assessment determines if you have limited capability for work.</p></li><li><p>Some descriptors give automatic LCWRA if met, particularly where a disability or health condition is severe and long-term.</p></li><li><p>For the new Severe Conditions Criteria, a claimant must meet at least one descriptor "constantly" rather than "most of the time" to qualify for the higher rate.</p></li></ul><p>LCWRA payments normally start after a 3-month waiting period from when you first provided continuous medical evidence (fit notes), unless you qualify under special rules - for example, terminal illness. You may receive LCWRA immediately if terminally ill.</p><blockquote><p>Keep copies of medical reports, test results and diaries of daily difficulties to support both the Capability for Work questionnaire and any appeals.</p></blockquote><img src="/images/blog/813fef4f-9a80-4f18-bf23-fc1c75038958.png" alt="A person is sitting at a desk, focused on writing notes in a journal, with various medical documents spread nearby. The scene suggests a connection to financial support, possibly related to disability benefits or employment and support allowance, as the individual prepares to provide medical evidence for their claim.">

## Medical Evidence, Fit Notes, and the Work Capability Assessment (WCA)

<p>Medical evidence underpins every sickness-related benefit. Fit notes and the WCA are central to both ESA and Universal Credit decisions, and you must provide medical evidence for the Work Capability Assessment.</p><p><strong>What is a fit note?</strong></p><ul><li><p>Issued by a GP, hospital doctor or other authorised medical professional.</p></li><li><p>States whether you are "not fit for work" or "may be fit for work" with adjustments.</p></li><li><p>Must cover specific dates, be signed (or digitally authenticated), and include the healthcare professional's details.</p></li></ul><p><strong>Evidence rules:</strong></p><ul><li><p>Self-certification for the first 7 days of sickness.</p></li><li><p>From day 8, a fit note or equivalent medical certificate is required for both ESA and Universal Credit sickness claims.</p></li><li><p>When moving from ESA to Universal Credit, you may sometimes continue under existing ESA evidence without new fit notes until a new WCA is completed.</p></li></ul><p><strong>The Work Capability Assessment process:</strong></p><ul><li><p>A Work Capability Assessment checks your ability to work. You may need a Work Capability Assessment if unfit for work over 28 days.</p></li><li><p>You complete the Capability for Work questionnaire (ESA50 or UC50), where you explain how your disability affects daily activities and work.</p></li><li><p>You can send additional medical evidence like letters from specialists, treatment plans, scans, and occupational therapy reports. It is always wise to provide medical evidence beyond the minimum.</p></li><li><p>The assessment can be in person, by video call, or phone, where a health professional asks questions and may observe how you manage certain tasks.</p></li></ul><p><strong>Outcomes</strong> link back to LCW and LCWRA as described above. Some people will not need a face-to-face assessment if submitted evidence is clear enough.</p><p><strong>If a decision finds you "fit for work" but you disagree:</strong></p><ul><li><p>You can ask for a mandatory reconsideration within one month of the decision date.</p></li><li><p>If still unhappy, you can appeal to an independent tribunal within strict time limits.</p></li><li><p>Continue providing fit notes and meeting any claimant commitment conditions while challenging, to avoid interruptions in payment.</p></li></ul>

## Support If You're Nearing the End of Life

<p>There are special rules, often called "special rules for end of life," if a medical professional believes you may have 12 months or less to live. These rules exist to make the process faster and less burdensome during an extremely difficult time.</p><p><strong>The role of medical evidence:</strong></p><ul><li><p>A clinician (usually your GP or consultant) completes an SR1 form confirming your diagnosis and treatment plan.</p></li><li><p>This is sent directly to the DWP or provided to a Jobcentre.</p></li></ul><p><strong>Advantages under special rules:</strong></p><ul><li><p>Faster decisions and minimal or no requirement for a Work Capability Assessment.</p></li><li><p>Automatic treatment as having limited capability for work and work-related activity (LCWRA) in Universal Credit and support group status in ESA.</p></li><li><p>No requirement for ongoing fit notes in many cases.</p></li></ul><p><strong>Impact on payments:</strong></p><ul><li><p>Higher rate LCWRA (£429.80 monthly) is paid from the outset. You may receive LCWRA immediately if terminally ill.</p></li><li><p>Work search and claimant commitment requirements are usually waived.</p></li><li><p>Other benefits affected include PIP, attendance allowance, and DLA for children.</p></li></ul><blockquote><p>If you think these rules might apply to you or someone you care for, speak with your healthcare team and a specialist benefits adviser as soon as possible.</p></blockquote>

## Other Financial Support When You Have a Health Condition

<p>Sickness benefits like ESA and Universal Credit are only part of the picture. People with a disability or health condition may qualify for more money or help with specific costs.</p><p><strong>Key additional benefits:</strong></p><ul><li><p>Personal independence payment (PIP) for people aged 16 to State Pension age who have difficulties with the daily living component or mobility. PIP supports those with long-term health issues, including a lifelong health condition.</p></li><li><p>Disability Living Allowance (DLA) for children under 16 and Adult Disability Payment in Scotland where applicable.</p></li><li><p>Attendance allowance for people over State Pension age who need help with personal care or supervision.</p></li></ul><p><strong>Support for carers:</strong></p><ul><li><p>Carer's Allowance if someone spends at least 35 hours a week caring for you and you receive certain disability benefits.</p></li><li><p>Additional carer elements within Universal Credit for people who care for someone with a disability that limits daily life.</p></li></ul><p><strong>Help linked to low income:</strong></p><ul><li><p>Council tax Reduction from your local authority, which can reduce or eliminate your council tax bill.</p></li><li><p>Help with rent through the housing costs element of Universal Credit.</p></li><li><p>Pension credit for older people on a low income.</p></li><li><p>A severe disability premium may apply in some legacy benefit situations for people living alone with a qualifying disability.</p></li></ul><p><strong>Help with health and travel costs:</strong></p><ul><li><p>Free prescriptions, dental treatment and eyesight tests if you receive certain benefits or meet income criteria.</p></li><li><p>Help with travel costs to NHS treatment under the Healthcare Travel Costs Scheme.</p></li></ul><p>Income Protection Insurance is a private option that pays 50–70% of your salary if unable to work due to illness, and can supplement state benefits.</p><blockquote><p>Use benefits calculators and local advice organisations to check for less obvious entitlements, including discretionary grants and local welfare schemes. Even a small extra monthly payment or discount can make a real difference to your living costs.</p></blockquote><img src="/images/blog/90e84380-616a-454e-b5e9-bf61b9406722.png" alt="A family is gathered around a dining table, reviewing various documents together, possibly related to financial support like employment and support allowance or universal credit claims. ## Common Mistakes and How to Avoid Them

<p>Many people miss out on sickness benefits or face delays because of avoidable errors.</p><p><strong>Key mistakes:</strong></p><ul><li><p>Not reporting a health condition or disability when first claiming Universal Credit, leading to lost backdating for LCWRA.</p></li><li><p>Failing to provide fit notes from day 8 onwards, causing a gap in medical evidence and delayed Work Capability Assessments.</p></li><li><p>Returning Capability for Work questionnaires late or incomplete, which can result in decisions made with limited information.</p></li></ul><p><strong>Common misunderstandings:</strong></p><ul><li><p>Assuming you cannot claim ESA or Universal Credit while working part-time, when in fact some work is allowed depending on the benefit and your capability.</p></li><li><p>Thinking that PIP or attendance allowance are only for people who use a wheelchair, rather than anyone whose other health conditions affect daily living or mobility. A disability affects people in many different ways.</p></li></ul><p><strong>Best practice:</strong></p><ul><li><p>Keep copies of all forms, fit notes and medical reports.</p></li><li><p>Use your online journal to record changes in your health condition.</p></li><li><p>Seek advice promptly if you receive a decision you do not agree with, as there are strict time limits for challenges. Citizens advice and welfare rights services are free and can make a significant difference.</p></li></ul>

## Top Tips for Maximising Your Sickness Benefit Entitlement

<p>Here is practical, proactive advice to make sure you receive all the financial support you are eligible for.</p><p><strong>Organisational tips:</strong></p><ul><li><p>Keep a simple timeline of your illness, fit notes and key DWP letters.</p></li><li><p>Maintain a folder (paper or digital) with all medical evidence and benefit correspondence.</p></li></ul><p><strong>Application tips:</strong></p><ul><li><p>Answer Capability for Work questionnaires in detail, focusing on your worst days and how long tasks take, not just whether you can do them once.</p></li><li><p>Ask healthcare professionals to include functional impact (for example, walking distance, concentration, fatigue) in clinic letters where possible. This helps with both the health assessment and any appeal.</p></li></ul><p><strong>Checking overlapping support:</strong></p><ul><li><p>Use benefits calculators to see if you can claim PIP, attendance allowance, Carer's Allowance or council tax Reduction in addition to sickness benefits.</p></li><li><p>Review whether new style ESA plus Universal Credit will leave you better off than Universal Credit alone, especially where household income is a factor.</p></li></ul><p><strong>Regular reviews:</strong></p><ul><li><p>Revisit your benefits after major health changes, new diagnoses or hospital stays.</p></li><li><p>Notify DWP quickly if your condition deteriorates, as this can justify a new work capability assessment and possibly LCWRA status or a higher rate of disability benefits.</p></li></ul>

## Frequently Asked Questions about Sickness Benefits



### Can I work while getting sickness benefits like ESA or Universal Credit with LCW/LCWRA?

<p>Limited work is sometimes allowed. New style ESA permits some "permitted work" up to set weekly earnings and hours limits - check the current 2026 limits as these change. Under Universal Credit, there is no fixed hours limit, but earnings reduce your award according to the taper rate and may trigger a review of your work capability if work becomes substantial.</p><p>You must always report any work and earnings to DWP. Doing suitable, part-time work does not automatically mean you lose LCW or LCWRA status, but failure to report work can lead to overpayments and possible penalties.</p>

### How long does it take to get a Work Capability Assessment decision?

<p>Timescales vary by area and workload, but many people wait several months from first submitting fit notes to receiving a WCA outcome. During this assessment period:</p><ul><li><p>Universal Credit is usually paid at the standard rate, and some claimants may be temporarily treated as having LCW.</p></li><li><p>New style ESA may be paid at an assessment rate until a decision is made.</p></li></ul><p>Providing complete forms and strong medical evidence as early as possible can help avoid unnecessary delays. Contact the assessment provider or DWP for updates if the wait becomes excessive.</p>

### What happens if the DWP decides I am fit for work but I disagree?

<p>You have the right to challenge the decision through a two-stage process. First, request a mandatory reconsideration within one month of the decision date, giving reasons and submitting extra evidence if possible. If still unhappy, lodge an appeal to an independent tribunal within the stated deadline.</p><p>Seek help from a welfare rights adviser, citizens advice or another specialist organisation to improve the quality of your challenge. Continue providing fit notes and meeting any claimant commitment conditions while challenging to avoid interruptions in payment.</p>

### Can I get both new style ESA and Universal Credit at the same time?

<p>Yes. You can receive new style employment and support allowance and Universal Credit together if you meet the conditions for each. New style ESA (sometimes referred to as style employment and support allowance ESA) is based on national insurance contributions and is not affected by savings or your partner's income. Universal Credit is means tested, and any ESA you receive is deducted pound-for-pound from your universal credit payment.</p><p>Claiming both can still be worthwhile, as ESA continues to be paid even if Universal Credit stops due to increased earnings or changes in household circumstances. This can provide additional help and extra money during transitional periods.</p>

### Do I always need to keep sending fit notes once I have LCW or LCWRA?

<p>In many cases, once a Work Capability Assessment has decided you have LCW or LCWRA, you will not need to continue providing fit notes unless DWP explicitly asks for new evidence. Before the WCA decision, you usually must supply continuous fit notes, otherwise your claim could be closed or delayed.</p><p>There are exceptions - people under special rules for end of life normally do not have to send further fit notes after the SR1 form is accepted. Check any letters or online journal messages carefully, as DWP will confirm when fit notes are no longer required for your life and circumstances.</p>
