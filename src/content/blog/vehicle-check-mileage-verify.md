A quick vehicle check mileage report can reveal whether the car mileage on the dashboard matches what official records say, helping you avoid clocked vehicles and overpaying for a car that has covered far more miles than advertised.</p>

## What Is a Vehicle Mileage Check?

<p>A vehicle mileage check is a report that shows all recorded odometer readings for a UK vehicle over time, letting you verify whether the mileage shown on the dashboard is genuine.</p><p>The core of any UK mileage check is MOT mileage from the DVSA database. Government and official databases are the most accessible way to verify vehicle mileage, and mileage data is sourced directly from the DVSA's MOT database. This is backed by basic DVLA data covering registration, keeper history, and vehicle specifications.</p><p>More complete mileage check data often includes private-sector sources such as service history entries, leasing returns, auction records, and dealer inspection readings. These additional data points help build a fuller picture of the vehicle's mileage beyond what MOT records alone can show.</p><p>A vehicle check mileage report helps confirm whether the current odometer reading is correct mileage or has been reduced. Most tools only need the registration number to start. No V5C or sign up required for a basic free check.</p>

## How to Check a UK Vehicle's Mileage by Registration

<p>Checking a car's mileage history takes just a few minutes. Here is the process:</p><ol><li><p><strong>Enter your reg</strong> into an online mileage check or car history check tool. Simply enter the registration number of the UK vehicle you want to investigate.</p></li><li><p><strong>Review the instant report.</strong> The tool pulls mot mileage history from DVSA, showing dates, test centres, and odometer readings for each MOT since the first test.</p></li><li><p><strong>Compare year-on-year readings.</strong> Confirm that the recorded mileage always increases at a realistic rate. For most privately used cars, 8,000 to 12,000 miles per year is typical.</p></li><li><p><strong>Upgrade if needed.</strong> A paid report can reveal additional mileage check data between MOTs, such as servicing, leasing returns, and trade inspections.</p></li><li><p><strong>Save or print the report.</strong> When viewing a car in person, cross-referencing the dashboard odometer with official records can verify mileage accuracy. Bring the report with you.</p></li></ol><blockquote><p>Always compare the mileage readings on the report with the current dashboard figure before agreeing to anything.</p></blockquote>

## Spotting Mileage Issues and Odometer Fraud

<p>Mileage clocking is a common fraud affecting 1 in 16 used cars in the UK. Separately, 1 in 18 cars in the UK have altered mileage, and 6.47% of checked vehicles had mileage discrepancies when checked against official data. Over 3 million UK vehicles have reduced mileage, making this a widespread problem.</p><p><strong>Common signs of mileage issues include:</strong></p><ul><li><p>Sudden drops in recorded miles between MOT tests (a later test shows fewer miles than an earlier one)</p></li><li><p>Unusually low mileage between consecutive MOTs, such as only 500 miles over an entire year on a daily driver</p></li><li><p>Missing years in the mot records or resets after plate changes</p></li><li><p>General wear and tear that indicates higher usage than shown by the odometer, for example heavily worn pedals, steering wheel, or seat bolsters on a car claiming 30,000 miles</p></li></ul><p><strong>How to detect odometer fraud:</strong></p><p>Plot each mot mileage reading against its test date. A backward step is a major red flag. Signs of odometer tampering include discrepancies between dashboard mileage and ECU mileage, since the Engine Control Unit usually stores the true, unmodified mileage.</p><p>Mileage clocking often occurs just before resale or after a PCP or lease agreement to avoid excess mileage penalties. Not every discrepancy is deliberate fraud; some result from MOT tester data entry errors or legitimate instrument cluster replacements. However, any unexplained mileage anomaly should be treated as a major red flag and investigated with extra documentation before purchase.</p><p>Buyers of clocked vehicles overpay by an average of around 48.8%, making this one of the costliest traps in the used car market.</p><img src="/images/blog/c8432fea-c7e0-45f0-9e53-bd71fb573048.png" alt="The image shows a close-up view of a car's dashboard, prominently displaying the odometer with clear mileage readings. ## When MOT Mileage Data Is Missing or Limited

<p>Several situations can leave a vehicle with no or limited mileage history in official mot records.</p><p><strong>Common cases include:</strong></p><ul><li><p>Vehicles under three years old (mileage data may be unavailable for vehicles under three years old because no MOT is required)</p></li><li><p>Brand-new 2025 or 2026 registrations that have not yet reached their first MOT</p></li><li><p>Fresh imports with mileage recorded under foreign systems</p></li><li><p>Long-term SORN vehicles that have not been tested</p></li></ul><p>Lack of mot data does not mean the car has low mileage. It simply means no official tests have been recorded yet. For these vehicles:</p><ul><li><p>Request a full service history, stamped booklets, digital dealer records, and invoices showing mileage at each visit.</p></li><li><p>Cross-check dealer service history against any available independent mileage data from history check tools.</p></li><li><p>Verify that advertised mileage (for example, 38,000 miles in June 2026) matches both the odometer and any dated repair or service bills.</p></li></ul>

## What to Do If You Find a Mileage Discrepancy

<p>When mileage anomalies appear in a vehicle check, take these steps:</p><ol><li><p><strong>Re-check mot history</strong> directly on the official UK government service to rule out display glitches in third-party tools. Look at the car's mot history carefully, comparing mot status, test dates, and mileage readings.</p></li><li><p><strong>Contact the test centre.</strong> If the wrong mileage is on a recent MOT, you should report incorrect mileage to the MOT centre within 28 days to request a correction.</p></li><li><p><strong>Escalate to DVSA.</strong> After 28 days, issues must be raised with DVSA, supported by evidence such as previous MOTs and service records. Gather evidence including stamped service booklets, invoices, and photographs.</p></li><li><p><strong>Ask the seller for documentation.</strong> Request written explanations and receipts for any cluster replacements, ECU swaps, or other work that could explain the discrepancy.</p></li><li><p><strong>Walk away if necessary.</strong> If the seller cannot convincingly explain inconsistent mileage records or refuses to share supporting documents, this is a major red flag. The informed decision is to walk away and keep looking.</p></li></ol><blockquote><p>A clocked car is never a bargain, no matter how attractive the price looks.</p></blockquote>

## Mileage Check FAQs



### Is a free MOT mileage check enough when buying a used car?

<p>A free mileage check is a strong starting point because it shows official odometer readings recorded at each mot test. For many everyday purchases, this free check of the vehicle's mot history is sufficient to spot obvious clocking or mileage tampered vehicles.</p><p>However, for higher-value cars or complex histories with multiple owners, UK buyers should also run a paid car history check. This reveals outstanding finance, write-offs, theft markers, and extra mileage data from trade sources. Using both mot history and an independent mileage report is recommended for cars registered before 2019 that may have had several keepers and more miles on the clock.</p>

### What is considered "high mileage" for a UK vehicle in 2026?

<p>Many UK cars average 8,000 to 12,000 miles per year. A 2020-registered car in 2026 with 60,000 to 70,000 miles would be typical. For motorway-heavy diesels, 120,000 miles can be perfectly reasonable if supported by consistent service records and mot mileage records.</p><p>"High mileage" is relative. Condition, service history, and consistent mileage history matter more than the absolute number. A well-maintained car with more miles can be a better buy than a neglected one claiming fewer miles.</p>

### Can a seller legally adjust the mileage on a car in the UK?

<p>Adjusting mileage is legal if disclosed when selling. For example, replacing an instrument cluster or ECU may result in a different odometer reading, and this is lawful provided the change is documented and declared to any future buyer.</p><p>It becomes criminal fraud under the Consumer Protection from Unfair Trading Regulations 2008 and the Fraud Act 2006 if a seller knowingly misrepresents the vehicle's mileage or fails to declare that it has been mileage tampered. Penalties can include fines and imprisonment. Buyers should insist on written confirmation if the mileage has ever been adjusted, and keep copies of all vehicle check mileage reports.</p>

### How often should I run a mileage check on a car I already own?

<p>Run a full vehicle check when buying, selling, or transferring the car, and whenever significant paperwork changes occur such as a plate change. Most owners of everyday UK vehicles do not need monthly checks, but annual verification around MOT time helps spot recording errors early.</p><p>Fleet operators and high-mileage business users may choose more regular checks as part of compliance and record-keeping policies.</p>

### Does a complete service history guarantee the mileage is genuine?

<p>A full, verifiable service history strongly supports genuine mileage but is not an absolute guarantee on its own. Always check that service mileage entries line up with the car's mileage history from mot records, invoices, and the current odometer reading.</p><p>Be cautious if service stamps appear out of sequence, are handwritten without garage details, or are clustered suspiciously close to the sale date. Cross-referencing multiple data sources remains the most reliable way to confirm the true mileage of any UK vehicle.</p>
