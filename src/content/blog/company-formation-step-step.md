## Introduction to UK Company Formation

<p>Company formation in the UK is the process of incorporating a limited company through Companies House, creating a separate legal entity that can own assets, enter contracts, and operate independently from its founders. At its core, forming a company requires transitioning from an idea to a legally compliant entity capable of trading, hiring, and raising capital.</p><p>This guide is aimed at both UK residents and non UK residents who want to register a company in England, Wales, Scotland, or Northern Ireland. Whether you are launching a side project or building a venture-backed startup, the company registration process follows the same core steps.</p><p>If you are weighing up whether to start as a sole trader or jump straight into a limited company, here is the key difference. A sole trader is the simplest business structure with minimal setup costs, but sole traders face unlimited liability for business debts, meaning personal assets are at risk. A limited company, by contrast, offers limited liability protection and is treated as a legally separate entity for tax, contracts, and debt.</p><p>Most readers here will be forming a private company limited by shares, but other limited company structures exist, including companies limited by guarantee, limited liability partnerships, and community interest companies. This article walks through the entire company formation process, how to choose a company formation agent, opening a business bank account, ongoing compliance, and common company formation FAQs.</p>

## 1. Preparing to Register a Company

<p>Good preparation speeds up the company formation application and dramatically reduces the risk of Companies House rejection. Before you file anything, you need to make several core decisions.</p><p>Key steps include validating a market need and creating a structured business plan. A comprehensive business plan should detail the company's mission and financial projections, and market research is essential to validate demand for a product or service before investing time and money in incorporation.</p><p>Here is a checklist of decisions to finalise before starting:</p><ul><li><p><strong>Company name</strong> - must be unique and comply with Companies House rules</p></li><li><p><strong>Company structure</strong> - private limited by shares is the most common choice</p></li><li><p><strong>Registered office address</strong> - a UK address where official mail is delivered</p></li><li><p><strong>Company director(s)</strong> - at least one human director required</p></li><li><p><strong>Shareholders and share capital</strong> - a limited company must issue at least one share upon registration</p></li><li><p><strong>SIC codes</strong> - standard industry classification codes reflecting your planned business activities</p></li><li><p><strong>Persons with significant control (PSCs)</strong> - anyone holding over 25% of shares or voting rights</p></li></ul><p>Companies House will place most of these company details on the public register, including director names, the company's registered office address, shareholding structure, and PSC information. However, directors can use a service address to shield their residential address from public view.</p><blockquote><p>Think of it this way: if you were a founder in 2026 planning to launch an e-commerce brand in Q4, locking in all these decisions before you even touch the incorporation form means you can complete the process in hours, not weeks.</p></blockquote>

## 2. Choosing a Company Name

<p>Your company name is the first thing customers, suppliers, and investors see. It also has strict legal requirements when you register your company.</p><p>You need a unique company name to register. Companies House applies a "same as" and "too like" test, ignoring punctuation and common words like "the." Before paying any fees, use an instant online company name search tool to confirm availability. The companies house website has a free name checker, and most company formation agents provide their own version.</p><p>Here are the checks to perform before locking in your preferred company name:</p><ul><li><p>The name must end with "Limited" or "Ltd" (or the Welsh equivalent)</p></li><li><p>It cannot be identical or confusingly similar to an existing registered name</p></li><li><p>Sensitive or restricted words like "Royal," "Bank," "Insurance," or "Chartered" require prior approval from the relevant government body or regulator</p></li><li><p>The name must not be offensive or suggest a misleading connection to government</p></li><li><p>Intellectual property should be secured to protect branding and products, so consider a separate trademark application through the UK IPO</p></li></ul><p>There is an important distinction between your registered company name and a trading name. For example, "North Shore Retail Ltd" could trade as "ShoreWear." The legal name appears on company documents and official filings, while the trading name is used for branding. Trading names cannot include "Limited" or "Ltd" unless the entity is actually a limited company.</p>

## 3. Understanding Limited Company Structures

<p>There are several limited company structures available in the UK, and choosing the right legal structure dictates personal liability and tax obligations. Each business structure also affects the startup's funding opportunities, so this decision deserves careful thought.</p><p>Here are the main types:</p><ul><li><p><strong>Private company limited by shares</strong> - the standard choice for small businesses and startups. Shareholders are personally liable only up to the value of their unpaid shares. Over 95% of registered companies in the UK are private limited by shares.</p></li><li><p><strong>Private company limited by guarantee</strong> - has no share capital; members guarantee a fixed amount instead. Companies limited by guarantee are often used by non-profits, charities, and membership organisations. This is sometimes called a guarantee company.</p></li><li><p><strong>Limited liability partnership (LLP)</strong> - a hybrid structure combining partnership flexibility with limited liability. Limited liability partnerships require at least two designated partners. A general partnership, by contrast, shares ownership and responsibilities among two or more people but does not offer liability protection.</p></li><li><p><strong>Community interest company (CIC)</strong> - designed for social enterprises that serve the community. Community interest companies cannot distribute profits as dividends, which is why they suit mission-driven organisations rather than profit-focused ventures. A certified b corporation, while different from a CIC, also signals social responsibility, but it is a certification rather than a legal structure.</p></li></ul><p>For most founders in 2026, a private company limited by shares makes sense. A limited company provides limited liability protection for shareholders, can own assets and enter contracts independently, can benefit from potential tax savings through lower corporation tax rates and dividend planning, and a limited company enhances credibility with clients and suppliers.</p><p>Legal and tax implications vary based on the chosen business structure, so if you are unsure, seek professional advice before filing.</p><img src="/images/blog/d32bce92-404a-4c4c-ae09-4e2c412e4f12.png" alt="A small group of business professionals is shaking hands in a modern office, symbolizing a new partnership or company launch, which reflects the dynamics of company formation and collaboration in a corporate environment. ## 4. Key People and Addresses: Company Officers and Company Details

<p>Companies House requires clear company details for transparency and anti-fraud purposes. This means accurately declaring company officers, ownership, and official addresses at the point of incorporation.</p><p>Company officers include the company director (at least one is mandatory) and, optionally, a company secretary. Directors must be at least 16 years old to register a company. Persons with significant control are individuals who hold more than 25% of shares, more than 25% of voting rights, or who have the right to appoint or remove a majority of directors.</p><p>For each director and shareholder, Companies House requires:</p><ul><li><p>Full legal name</p></li><li><p>Date of birth (month and year are public; the day is restricted)</p></li><li><p>Residential address (kept confidential unless specifically requested)</p></li><li><p>Service address (this is the public-facing address)</p></li><li><p>Nationality and occupation</p></li><li><p>Shareholding details</p></li></ul><p>A registered office address is required for all UK companies. This is the official address where statutory correspondence from Companies House and HMRC is delivered. A UK registered company requires a UK address, and this address is part of the public register.</p><p>Many founders use a company formation agent's registered office service or a virtual business address to protect their home address privacy. Public records can lead to increased regulatory scrutiny and transparency for companies, so keeping a residential address off the register is a practical concern, not just a preference.</p>

## 5. Step‑by‑Step Company Formation Process

<p>This is the core section. Here is how to form a company in the UK in 2026, from start to finish.</p><p>There are three main ways to register:</p><ul><li><p><strong>Directly with Companies House online</strong> - through their web filing service</p></li><li><p><strong>By post</strong> - using Form IN01 (paper application)</p></li><li><p><strong>Via an authorised company formation agent</strong> - who files electronically on your behalf</p></li></ul><p>The typical steps, in order:</p><ol><li><p>Verify identity for all directors and PSCs (mandatory since November 2025)</p></li><li><p>Choose your company structure</p></li><li><p>Confirm your preferred company name is available</p></li><li><p>Complete the incorporation form: directors, shareholders, SIC codes, articles of association</p></li><li><p>Pay the companies house fee</p></li><li><p>Wait for approval</p></li></ol><p>Companies House charges a £100 registration fee for all companies. Company registration typically takes one to five days, though most standard electronic filings are processed within 24 hours, subject to companies house workload. Paper filings using Form IN01 take eight to ten working days. Same day incorporation services are available for an additional fee of £156 through approved software providers.</p><p>After approval, the founder receives a certificate of incorporation, a unique company number, and an authentication code used for future filings on the companies house website.</p>

### 5.1 Digital vs Postal Company Registration in 2026

<p>Digital company registration is faster, cheaper, and less prone to errors than posting a paper form. Here is how the two compare:</p><ul><li><p><strong>Online filing</strong> - the companies house filing fee is £100, processing typically within 24 hours, and identity checks are handled electronically</p></li><li><p><strong>Postal filing</strong> - costs £124, takes eight to ten working days, and requires physical documents to be mailed</p></li></ul><p>Consider this scenario: an entrepreneur files online on a Monday morning, passes identity verification instantly, and receives their certificate of incorporation by Tuesday. They open a business bank account the same week and start trading. Compare this to a founder who posts Form IN01, waits over a week, then still has to set up banking and compliance.</p><blockquote><p>Digital company formation is usually preferable because it is faster, cheaper, and allows proof of ID to be handled electronically. Unless there is a specific reason to use paper, go digital.</p></blockquote><p>Most company formation agents file electronically, which means you get the speed benefit without needing to navigate the Companies House system yourself.</p>

### 5.2 Identity Verification and Compliance Checks

<p>Since 18 November 2025, every new company director and PSC must complete identity verification before or during incorporation. This is a legal requirement under the Economic Crime and Corporate Transparency Act 2023.</p><p>There are three main verification routes:</p><ul><li><p><strong>GOV.UK One Login</strong> - using the ID check app, webcam, or a face-to-face appointment at a Post Office</p></li><li><p><strong>Through an authorised corporate service provider (ACSP)</strong> - a registered agent, law firm, or accountant who verifies identity under anti-money laundering rules</p></li><li><p><strong>Via your chosen company formation agent's secure formation service portal</strong> - many agents handle this as part of their company formation packages</p></li></ul><p>What to prepare:</p><ul><li><p>A valid passport or driving licence (photographic ID)</p></li><li><p>Proof of current address</p></li><li><p>Personal details: full name, date of birth, nationality</p></li><li><p>For non UK residents: identity verification is required for non-UK residents forming a company, and some routes may not accept all international documents, so the ACSP route is often easier</p></li></ul><p>Anti-money laundering and know-your-customer checks are built into the registration process. These are not optional extras; they are part of companies house authorisation requirements in 2026.</p>

## 7. Opening a Business Bank Account

<p>Every new company should open a separate business bank account, even though it is not always strictly mandatory by law. Using a dedicated business account helps demonstrate that the limited company is a separate legal entity with its own funds.</p><p>Separating personal and business finances simplifies bookkeeping, tax returns, and proving limited liability if there is ever a dispute. If personal assets are mixed with company funds in a single personal account, a court could question whether the company is truly a legally separate entity.</p><p>In 2026, typical documents required by UK banks and fintechs include:</p><ul><li><p>Certificate of incorporation</p></li><li><p>Company details (registered number, SIC codes, registered office)</p></li><li><p>ID for directors and PSCs</p></li><li><p>Sometimes a simple business plan</p></li></ul><p>Many company formation agents now partner with business banking partners and provide referrals from within the formation order journey. In 2026, the VAT registration threshold sits at around £90,000, and PAYE registration is required if hiring staff.</p>

## 10. Company Formation FAQs

<p>Below are answers to common questions that go beyond the main article content and reflect typical concerns for founders in 2026.</p>

### Do I Need to Be in the UK to Form a Limited Company?

<p>No. Physical presence in the UK is not required in 2026. Non-UK residents can register a UK company online from overseas. The company still needs a UK registered office address, and identity checks must be completed for directors and PSCs using accepted documents.</p><p>Be aware that tax residence and where profits are actually generated may affect where tax is due. If you are a non-UK founder, take professional tax advice to understand your obligations in both the UK and your home country. Some banks and business account providers have additional residency rules even though Companies House does not.</p>

### Can I Run More Than One Business Under One Limited Company?

<p>Yes. A single limited company can operate multiple business lines using different trading names, as long as all activities are declared in the accounts and tax returns. Companies House requires at least one SIC code on incorporation, but additional codes can be added later to reflect new activities.</p><p>The advantages include simpler administration and lower formation costs. The downsides include more complex accounting and shared liability if one business line runs into problems. If activities diverge significantly - for example, a software consultancy and a food delivery service - forming a second limited company might be more appropriate.</p>

### What Happens If I Form a Company but Don't Start Trading?

<p>A company can be incorporated and remain dormant until the owner is ready to trade. Dormant companies still have obligations: filing a confirmation statement and dormant accounts with Companies House each year.</p><p>If the company has never traded, there may be no corporation tax return required, but HMRC must be kept informed of its dormant status. If the founder decides not to use the company at all, they can apply to strike it off the register, following the correct legal process. Do not ignore Companies House letters, even when there is no trading activity.</p>

### Do I Need a Business Bank Account for a UK Limited Company?

<p>While the law does not always mandate a separate business bank account, it is strongly recommended and often expected by accountants and HMRC. Using a dedicated business bank account helps demonstrate that the limited company is a separate legal entity with its own funds.</p><p>Mixing personal and company money in a single personal account can cause bookkeeping problems and weaken limited liability arguments if the company faces a legal dispute. Research business banking partners early, ideally immediately after receiving the certificate of incorporation.</p>

### Can I Change My Company Name or Details After Formation?

<p>Yes. A company can change its company name, registered office address, directors, shareholders, and share structure after incorporation, following Companies House procedures. Name changes require passing a resolution and filing a specific form. Address and officer updates are filed as separate events online.</p><p>Changes usually appear on the public register within 24 to 48 hours for electronic filings, depending on workload. Banks, suppliers, and tax authorities will also need to be informed of major changes. Significant changes to share classes or articles of association should be made with professional legal or accounting advice.</p>
