If you're on a low income or out of work, Universal Credit could help you cover essential living costs like rent, food, and bills. But applying for the first time can feel overwhelming. This guide breaks the process down into clear, manageable steps so you know exactly what to do, what documents you need, and what happens after you submit your universal credit application.

Key Takeaways

  • Most people must apply for universal credit online through GOV.UK. You need an email address, a bank account, and a mobile phone number to get started.

  • After creating your online account, you must complete a to do list of questions before your claim is assessed. You have 28 days to complete your application after creating an account.

  • Your first payment usually takes five weeks after applying, but you can request an advance payment if you need money sooner.

  • You might get extra money if you have children, childcare costs, a disability or health condition, or if you care for someone with a disability.

  • You must agree a claimant commitment with a work coach and keep your online journal up to date to keep being paid.

  • Before you claim universal credit, check if you're better off leaving your current benefits in place. Free support is available from Citizens Advice if you're unsure.

What Universal Credit Is and Who Can Claim

Universal Credit is a monthly payment from the government to help with living costs. It's designed for people of working age in England, Scotland, and Wales who are on a low income or out of work. As of 2026, it has fully replaced six legacy benefits for most working-age claimants. Single claimants receive £424.90 per month, while couples receive £666.97 per month as a standard allowance, with additional amounts possible depending on your circumstances.

Universal Credit replaces the following:

  • Income-based style jobseeker's allowance (JSA)

  • Income-related employment and support allowance (ESA)

  • Income Support

  • Working Tax Credit

  • Child Tax Credit

  • Housing benefit (for most working-age people)

Some other benefits remain completely separate and are not affected by a universal credit claim. These include personal independence payment (PIP), disability living allowance (DLA), carer's allowance, and child benefit. You can receive these alongside Universal Credit.

Basic eligibility at a glance:

  • You must be at least 16 years old (though most claimants are 18+, with specific exceptions for 16–17-year-olds who have children, disabilities, or need to live independently)

  • You must be under state pension age

  • You must normally live in the UK

  • Your income and savings must be low enough - you cannot claim universal credit if savings exceed £16,000

  • If savings are between £6,000 and £16,000, your payment is reduced

You may still be able to claim universal credit if you are in full-time education, have a disability or health condition, are in the armed forces, or live with a partner, but the rules are stricter in each case.

People in Northern Ireland claim Universal Credit through a different system and should check NI-specific guidance before applying.

Check If Universal Credit Is Right for You Before You Apply

This step is critical. Claiming Universal Credit usually stops your existing benefits and tax credits from the date of your claim. Even if your universal credit payment turns out to be less than what you were getting before, you normally cannot go back to your existing benefits once you've made the switch.

Check carefully before applying if any of these apply to you:

  • You or your partner currently receive pension credit

  • You get tax credits (Working Tax Credit or Child Tax Credit)

  • Your legacy benefits include a severe disability premium

  • You have variable earnings (for example, you're self employed or on zero-hours contracts)

  • You're approaching state pension age

Use official benefit calculators on GOV.UK or through services like Turn2us and Citizens Advice to work out how much universal credit you'd receive compared to your current support.

If the DWP sends you a migration notice with a deadline, you must claim Universal Credit by that date to keep your support and qualify for transitional protection. Missing the deadline could mean losing both your legacy benefits and any top-up payments you'd be entitled to.

If you're unsure whether Universal Credit is right for you, contact citizens advice or another local advice agency before making any changes. Getting this wrong could leave you worse off with no way back.

What You Need Ready Before You Apply for Universal Credit

Gathering the right documents before you start will save you time and prevent delays. Think of this as a practical checklist - have everything to hand before you open the online form.

Personal details:

  • Full legal name, date of birth

  • National Insurance number

  • Address history (current and previous)

  • Mobile phone number and email address

  • Your partner's name, date of birth, and NI number (if applicable)

Financial details:

  • Bank account or building society details (sort code and account number)

  • Recent payslips or P60

  • Details of any savings, investments, or private/workplace pensions

  • Bank statements if requested

  • Any redundancy pay or insurance plan payouts you've received

Housing information:

  • Your tenancy agreement or mortgage statement

  • Your landlord's details, including landlord's address

  • Rent amount in pounds and pence, including any service charges

  • Whether you have rent free weeks

  • Details of how many bedrooms your home has

  • Whether your landlord is a private landlord or housing association

To apply for universal credit, you should gather details about your housing and income before starting. You must provide details of your housing costs and rental agreement.

Family and childcare:

  • Names and dates of birth of children who live with you

  • Child benefit award details

  • Childcare provider registration or contract

  • Average monthly childcare costs with proof such as invoices

Health and caring responsibilities:

  • Information about any ongoing health conditions or disability affecting your ability to work

  • Details of disability benefits you or someone you care for receives

  • A fit note from your GP or hospital if you're currently off work sick

Having these documents ready will make completing the to do list on your universal credit account much quicker and help you avoid the delays that catch many new claimants out.

Bank Account and Payment Arrangements

Universal Credit is normally paid into a bank, building society, or credit union account. Universal credit payments are made monthly, or twice a month in Scotland if you request it. A bank account is required to claim universal credit.

Here's how payments work:

  • You should have an account in your own name, or a joint account if you're claiming with a partner

  • For a joint claim, only one bank account is needed for the payment, but both partners must enter their own bank details in their separate online accounts

  • For your first payment, you may be able to use a trusted friend's or family member's account temporarily, but you should open your own account as soon as possible

  • If you've been refused an account, ask the universal credit team about the Payment Exception Service so you can still receive payments while you sort things out

Never share bank account logins, PINs, or passwords in your online journal or with anyone who contacts you unexpectedly. The DWP will never ask for sensitive account information through your journal, texts, or unsolicited calls.

Step‑by‑Step: How to Apply for Universal Credit Online

You must apply for universal credit online. Most new claims are made through GOV.UK, and the process runs from account creation through to declaration. Here's what to expect at each stage.

Setting up your account:

  1. Go to the official Universal Credit page on GOV.UK

  2. Enter your email address and mobile number

  3. Create a secure password and answer memorable questions

  4. Your universal credit account is now active

If you live with a partner, both of you must create accounts on the same day and link them using the code provided to make it a joint claim.

Once you create accounts and start your claim, you enter basic details and are then given an online to do list of questions about your situation. You can save your progress and return later, but you have 28 days to complete your application after creating an account or it will expire and you'll need to start again.

If you cannot claim online because of a disability, language barrier, or lack of internet access, you can call the universal credit helpline or visit a Jobcentre to ask about making a claim by phone or with assisted digital support.

Completing Your Online 'To‑Do List'

Your to do list is where you answer questions that decide how much universal credit you get. You need to answer questions across several areas:

  • Nationality and immigration status

  • Who you live with (partner, children, non-dependants)

  • Housing and rent, including housing costs

  • Work and earnings (employed or self employed)

  • Savings and investments

  • Health conditions and disabilities

  • Children and childcare costs

  • Bank account details

Enter exact figures in pounds and pence, especially for rent, earnings, and childcare costs, to avoid under- or over-payments. If you're making a joint claim, both partners must complete their own sections and confirm the answers before the claim can be submitted.

Double-check everything before moving on. Some answers can't be changed until after you submit and contact your work coach. Incomplete to do lists are one of the most common causes of delays in getting your first payment.

Confirming Your Identity

Everyone who applies must confirm their identity online or at a Jobcentre before the universal credit claim can be fully processed. You must confirm your identity to complete your claim.

Typical documents for verifying your identity online include:

  • A uk passport

  • A full or provisional UK driving licence

  • A credit or debit card

  • Payslips or P60

You need to confirm your identity with specific documents. If online verification doesn't work, you'll be given another to do item to attend a Jobcentre appointment with physical documents such as a passport, birth certificate, or residence permit.

People from the EU, EEA, or Switzerland may also need proof of settled or pre-settled status under the EU Settlement Scheme. Complete identity checks as soon as possible - your first payment cannot be made until this step is finished.

Submitting Your Declaration

Once all questions are answered and your identity is confirmed, you must tick declarations to confirm everything is correct. Review each section, correct any errors, and confirm that you understand you must report when your circumstances change.

The date you submit your declaration is normally treated as the official date of your new claim. After submission, you can use your online journal to send messages to your work coach if you remember something you forgot to include.

Health, Disability and Fit Notes

If you have a disability or health condition that affects your ability to work or look for work, you should report it during your application. This includes any long-term physical or mental health condition.

You should provide a fit note (sometimes called a sick note) from your GP or hospital doctor. A fit note must include:

  • A start date

  • An expected end date or "ongoing" status

  • The signature of a healthcare professional

You must provide a fit note if ill for more than 7 days. A fit note is required if you have a long-term health condition. Upload a photo of it through your journal or take it to the Jobcentre.

Providing medical evidence of ongoing health conditions may lead to a Work Capability Assessment. If you're found to have limited capability for work or for work-related activity, you could receive extra money and have reduced work-search requirements. Note that from April 2026, the LCWRA element rates changed for new claimants, with the higher rate now more restricted.

A healthcare professional may need to review your case, and you may be asked to attend medical treatment reviews. Keep your online journal updated about your health, especially if your condition changes or you're admitted to hospital.

Children, Childcare Costs and Extra Money You Might Get

Universal Credit is made up of a standard allowance plus extra amounts depending on your situation. Here's what could increase your payment:

  • Children: You may get extra money for each dependent child living with you. Since 6 April 2026, the two-child limit has been removed, so you can now claim for all children regardless of birth date. You must provide evidence including dates of birth and child benefit awards.

  • Childcare: You can claim up to 85% of childcare costs if working and using a registered childcare provider such as a nursery, childminder, or after-school club. You must claim childcare costs by uploading invoices or payment confirmations as evidence. You need to provide evidence of childcare costs if applicable.

  • Disability: If you or a child you look after receives DLA, PIP, or you're assessed as having limited capability for work, additional support allowance elements apply.

  • Caring: If you care for someone for 35 hours or more per week who receives certain disability benefits, you may get a carer element. However, this can affect the benefits of the person you care for, so seek advice first.

To understand how much universal credit you could receive with all applicable elements, use a benefits calculator or speak to an adviser.

After You Apply: Claimant Commitment, First Payment and Ongoing Duties

Applying is only the first step. Before you receive your first universal credit payment, several things need to happen.

You may need to book an appointment with a work coach during the application process. At this appointment (at a Jobcentre or by phone), you agree your claimant commitment. This document sets out what you must do to look for work or prepare for work, and it's personalised based on your circumstances - whether you're working, have young children, are a student, or have a health condition.

You must accept your claimant commitment to receive payments. Failing to accept or follow it can lead to sanctions and reduced payments.

Your first payment is usually paid monthly, arriving about five weeks after the date you submit your claim. This covers one full assessment period (one calendar month) plus up to seven days for processing. Your second payment and all following payments then arrive on a regular monthly cycle.

In 2024–25, 56% of new UC claimants took out an advance to bridge the five-week wait. If you can't manage during this period, you can request an advance payment if needed. This is an interest-free loan repaid from future Universal Credit payments over up to 24 months.

Ongoing duties after your first payment:

  • Check your online journal and to do list regularly

  • Reply to messages from your work coach

  • Attend all booked appointments

  • Report any changes - new work, changes in rent, a new baby, or a change in health

  • Provide evidence when asked

Universal Credit does not automatically reduce your council tax bill. You usually need to make a separate claim for council tax reduction with your local council. Council tax support is handled entirely separately from your universal credit claim.

Getting Help with Your Universal Credit Claim

There is free support available if you're unsure how to apply for universal credit or find the online process difficult.

  • Universal Credit helpline: Call 0800 328 5644 for free support. Textphone and Relay UK services exist for people with hearing or speech difficulties. The customer service department can answer questions about your claim status and next steps.

  • Citizens Advice Help to Claim: This service assists with online applications and Jobcentre preparation, from checking eligibility right through to your first appointment. The Help to Claim service assists with online applications and jobcentre preparation.

  • Local organisations: Age UK, disability charities, and housing charities may help with filling in online forms, uploading documents, or understanding your claimant commitment. You can get support from Age UK for Universal Credit claims.

  • Jobcentre digital support: If you don't have a computer or smartphone, or if you find online applications difficult because of a disability or language barrier, Jobcentre staff can provide assisted digital support.

Seeking independent advice can prevent mistakes, missed deadlines, and sanctions on your universal credit claim. If you've received a migration notice, getting help promptly is especially important.

Universal Credit FAQs

Can I Backdate My Universal Credit Claim If I Apply Late?

In some situations you can ask for your claim to be backdated by up to one month if you had a good reason for not applying earlier - for example, serious illness, relationship breakdown, or an official error. You must request backdating through your online journal or by contacting the helpline as soon as possible, and you'll need to provide evidence to support your reasons. Backdating is not guaranteed and is decided on a case-by-case basis by the DWP.

What If I Don't Have an Email Address or Smartphone When I Apply?

You normally need an email address and mobile phone to set up and manage your universal credit account. If you don't have them, you can set up a free email address and use public computers at libraries or Jobcentres. If you still cannot manage online because of a disability, language barrier, or other difficulty, contact the universal credit helpline or visit a Jobcentre to ask for an alternative way to claim and manage your account.

Will Universal Credit Affect My Existing Disability Benefits?

Disability benefits like personal independence payment (PIP), disability living allowance (DLA), and Industrial Injuries Disablement Benefit are not replaced by Universal Credit. You can usually receive these alongside Universal Credit, and they may increase the amount you get through disability or carer elements. However, some severe disability premiums included in legacy benefits may stop when you move to Universal Credit, so get advice before claiming if you're unsure.

Can Students Claim Universal Credit?

Most full-time students cannot claim universal credit, but there are important exceptions. These include students with a child, certain young people aged 16–21 without parental support, and students assessed as having limited capability for work who receive specific disability benefits. Check the current rules or seek advice before applying if you are in further or higher education.

How Do I Challenge a Universal Credit Decision If I Think It's Wrong?

If you disagree with a decision - such as the amount of your award or a sanction - you can ask for a "mandatory reconsideration" within one month of the decision date. You can request this through your online journal, by letter, or by phone. Explain clearly what you think is wrong and provide any supporting evidence or medical evidence. If you're still unhappy after the reconsideration, you may be able to appeal to an independent tribunal. The following details should be included in your request: what decision you're challenging, why you believe it's incorrect, and any new evidence you have.
